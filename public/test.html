Test cricket

Test matches are the longest format: up to five days, four innings possible (each team can bat twice). There is no per-innings over cap—declarations, follow-ons, and weather interruptions are part of the chess match between captains.

Days and sessions

Each day typically has three sessions with breaks. The red ball (or pink under lights) swings and seams more than a white ball; pitches crack and spin more on days four and five. That creates ebbs and flows rare in shorter formats.

World Test Championship (WTC)

The ICC introduced a league cycle culminating in a final so Tests could crown a seasonal champion beyond ad hoc series wins. Bilateral tours accumulate points; the top teams meet in a final. It rewards consistency across conditions (home and away debate remains part of fan conversation).

Skills that define Tests

  • Batsmen — leaving the ball, long concentration, managing tired bowlers.
  • Pacers — reverse swing, disciplined lengths, multi-day workloads.
  • Spinners — exploiting rough patches and footmarks late in the match.
  • Keepers — standing up to spin or back for pace across hundreds of overs.

Pink-ball Tests

Day-night Tests use a pink ball for visibility under floodlights. Swing can behave differently; dew in some regions affects the evening session. They aim for crowds after work while preserving the five-day narrative.
