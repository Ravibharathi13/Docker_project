One Day International (ODI)

An ODI is a limited-overs match between countries with ICC status—each side bats once for a fixed number of overs (currently 50 per innings for standard men’s ODIs). It balances the patience of Test cricket with a single-day finish and clear tactics around powerplays and death overs.

Structure

  • Innings length — 50 overs unless reduced by rain (DLS may revise targets).
  • Powerplay — Fielding restrictions early overs encourage aggressive batting.
  • Middle overs — Spinners often control the rate; rotating strike matters.
  • Death overs — Late innings acceleration (overs 41–50) decides many games.

Why ODIs exist

Before T20, ODIs made cricket stadium-friendly in one day. They fuel World Cups, rankings, and qualification. White-ball specialists—finishers, pinch hitters, wrist-spinners—often shine here even if they play fewer Tests.

ODI vs T20 vs Test

ODI rewards building an innings and surviving a bad spell; T20 rewards risk and impact per ball; Test rewards endurance and adapting across days. Many modern players train for all three but peak differently by format.
