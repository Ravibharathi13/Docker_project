Domestic & franchise leagues

Leagues are privately or board-run competitions where city or regional franchises buy players through auctions or drafts. They usually play T20 (sometimes T10 or hybrid rules) under floodlights. They are not the same as bilateral international series—the teams carry brand names (e.g. city-based) rather than national colours.

Why leagues matter

Leagues widen the talent pool, give young players exposure next to global stars, and finance infrastructure. Scheduling must coexist with ICC windows and national team commitments, which is why you often see rested internationals or split squads during league seasons.

Examples by region

League Typical window Notes
Indian Premier League (IPL) March–May High-profile T20; eight to ten franchises; central to the global T20 calendar.
Big Bash League (BBL) Australian summer Family-friendly scheduling; innovation in rules (Power Surge, etc.).
Caribbean Premier League (CPL) Aug–Sep Showcases West Indian flair; overseas stars common.
Pakistan Super League (PSL) Feb–Mar Rotating venues; strong local following.
SA20 South African summer Franchise model aligned with South Africa’s white-ball reboot.
The Hundred (England) Jul–Aug 100-ball format; distinct from traditional T20.

Other examples include the Bangladesh Premier League (BPL), Lanka Premier League (LPL), and emerging leagues in the USA and UAE. Names and seasons can change—always check the current board announcement for dates.

Leagues vs international cricket

National teams represent countries under ICC rules. League teams are franchises licensed for a season. A player might captain their country on Sunday and open batting for a franchise on Wednesday—contracts and national duty shape who plays where.
