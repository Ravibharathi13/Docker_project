The game in a nutshell

Cricket is a bat-and-ball contest between two teams, usually of eleven players each. One side bats in innings, trying to score runs; the other bowls and fields, trying to take wickets and limit scoring. This site summarizes how the sport is structured around the world: domestic leagues, multi-team tournaments, ICC World Cups, and the three main international formats.

A very short history

Modern laws trace back to England in the 18th century. The sport spread through the British Empire and took deep roots in Australia, the Caribbean, South Asia, southern Africa, and New Zealand. Today the ICC Full Members, Associate nations, and thriving domestic leagues make cricket both a traditional five-day game and a global entertainment product under lights.

Key ideas

Innings

Teams alternate batting and bowling. In limited-overs cricket each innings has a fixed number of overs; in Tests each side can bat twice (or more) across days.

Runs & wickets

Batsmen score by hitting the ball and running, or reaching the boundary. The fielding side dismisses batsmen (bowled, caught, LBW, run out, etc.)—each dismissal is a wicket.
